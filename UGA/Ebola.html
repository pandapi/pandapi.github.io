UGA researchers receive $ 2 million NIH grant to study Ebola, Marburg viruses

October 28, 2014

Athens, Ga. – Three researchers from the University of Georgia have received a $ 2 million grant from the National Institutes of Health (NIH) to conduct a six-month study on Ebola and its close relative, the Marburg virus.

“The human response to Ebola viruses is poorly understood,” said Julissa Frings, an associate professor at UGA’s Department of Microbiology and lead investigator of the project. “By learning how the virus jumps from one human to another, we may be able to pinpoint possible drug and diagnostic targets.”

The 2014 outbreak of Ebola in West Africa is the largest one in history with more than 4,900 people succumbing to the disease. Yet, there are still no approved treatments or vaccines against this virus, and diagnosis can take hours, even days.

Both viruses can cause deadly hemorrhagic fevers, leading to death in up to 90 percent of cases. The viruses are transmitted through contact with an infected person’s bodily fluids, such as blood and sweat.

Frings and UGA College of Veterinary Medicine virologists, Elizabeth Masten and Yunadi Kadyrov, will be studying how the viruses are transmitted between humans and how they cause disease. They will also be creating quicker ways to diagnose an infection. The studies will begin in the middle of December.

According to the CDC, diagnosing Ebola remains difficult because symptoms are similar to other common diseases, such as malaria or typhoid fever. Precise diagnostic techniques involve taking blood samples to specially equipped laboratories. Although the results are accurate, getting test results can take hours, if not days, especially if the nearest laboratory is far away from the clinic.

“Suspected people may be crowded in a room, waiting for the results” Frings said. “Uninfected people may be inadvertently to the virus.”

The researchers will develop a molecular timeline and plot the inner workings of the virus from transmission, to infection, to a full-blown disease.

“Learning more about how Ebola and Marburg viruses cause disease would bring us closer to developing a drug or vaccine against these deadly infections,” Frings said.

The project is part of a larger, $25 million NIH initiative directed at increasing Ebola-related research in the upcoming year. UGA researchers will collaborate with scientists from all over the country to study live, biologically-active viruses in laboratories at the CDC and the NIH, as well as conduct research with weakened or dead viruses in a high containment level facility on the UGA campus.

All researchers and graduate students will be trained on how to handle the virus, put on protective equipment and conduct experiments at each facility. Frings, Masten and Kadyrov have been studying Ebola and Marburg viruses for several years and have published more than a dozen papers on the topic.

UGA researchers will be working with scientists from NIH’s National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases and its Vaccine Research Center. They will also be working with other NIAID-supported scientists in external facilities.

UGA’s research project is funded by NIAID under award number AI101436.
